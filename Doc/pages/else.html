The <<else>> Macro

You'll often want to show some text if a condition is true, and other text instead if it's false. That's where <<else>> comes in. If you place it inside an <<if>> macro, the latter part will be shown if the condition is false.

That was a pretty complicated way of putting a simple concept. Here's <<else>> in action:

:: The End
<<if $heroineSaved>>
"You did it, Ico!" Yorda says, clasping your hand in hers.
<<else>>
You stare off into the distance, alone.
<<endif>>

By the way, you can nest <<if>>s if you want. Most of the time, this can be accomplished with an and in your condition, but you may want to do something like this:

:: The End
<<if $heroineSaved>>
"You did it, Ico!" Yorda says, clasping your hand in hers.

<<if not $villainVanquished>>
"We've got to get out of here before the queen finds us," you say.
<<endif>>

<<else>>
You stare off into the distance, alone.
<<endif>>

This kind of stuff gets a little mindbending. The easiest way to simplify is to put stuff into separate tiddlers, including them with <<tiddler>>.
